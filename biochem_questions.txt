Q3 — SIP knowledge (biotech-specific):
"Explain what SIP (Steam-in-Place) sterilization is, why it is necessary before each fermentation batch, and why it might cause protein fouling on the fermenter jacket."
Expected answer:
SIP is the process of sterilizing the fermenter vessel and associated pipework in place by circulating pressurised steam (121°C, 15 psi minimum) for a defined hold time (typically 20-30 minutes). It is necessary to achieve sterility assurance level (SAL) of 10⁻⁶ before inoculation to prevent contamination of the culture. Protein fouling occurs because residual proteins from the previous batch or from media components in contact with the hot jacket surface denature at sterilization temperatures and deposit as a layer on the stainless steel surface. This is why cleaning (CIP) must precede sterilization (SIP) — incomplete CIP leaves protein residuals that then bake onto the surface during SIP.

Problem 10: Yogurt Production
Scenario
A dairy plant producing yogurt reports that the final product remains watery and does not develop the expected gel structure. The diagnostic platform reports slower lactic acid production than expected.

Q1 – Biological Understanding
Question
Why is lactic acid production important during yogurt manufacture?
Answer
Lactic acid lowers the pH of milk, causing casein proteins to coagulate and form the characteristic yogurt gel. Insufficient acid production prevents proper gel formation, resulting in a thin and watery product.
